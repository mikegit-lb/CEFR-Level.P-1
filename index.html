The Rise of Urban Farming

In recent years, urban farming has become an increasingly popular trend in cities around the world. With growing concerns about food security and environmental sustainability, more and more city dwellers are turning small rooftops, balconies, and even old warehouses into productive vegetable gardens.

Supporters argue that urban farms can reduce the distance food travels from farm to table, significantly lowering carbon emissions. Furthermore, growing your own food can be a meaningful way to reconnect with nature and build a stronger sense of community.

However, critics point out that urban farming faces serious challenges. Space and sunlight are limited in many cities, and the initial cost of setting up a garden can be quite high. Some experts also question whether small-scale urban farming can ever produce enough food to make a meaningful impact on a city's overall food supply. Despite these concerns, the movement continues to grow, driven by passionate volunteers and forward-thinking local governments.

The Psychology of Procrastination

Most people believe that procrastination is simply laziness — a lack of willpower or motivation. However, psychologists increasingly argue that this is a misconception. At its core, procrastination is an emotional regulation problem, not a time management one.

When faced with a task that feels overwhelming, boring, or anxiety-inducing, the human brain naturally seeks relief from that discomfort. Checking social media, watching another episode, or making yet another cup of coffee all offer an immediate escape from the unpleasant feeling associated with the challenging task. In the short term, this strategy works brilliantly — the discomfort disappears. However, the long-term consequences are often significant: missed deadlines, increased stress, and a growing sense of guilt and self-criticism.

Research by Dr. Timothy Pychyl and Dr. Fuschia Sirois suggests that self-compassion is one of the most effective tools for overcoming procrastination. Rather than punishing yourself harshly for delaying, treating yourself with the same kindness you would offer a struggling friend can break the cycle of negative emotions that fuel further avoidance. The irony, it turns out, is that being kinder to yourself may actually make you more productive.

Speaking Respond fluently and at length. Aim for 1–2 minutes per task.

Task 1 — Extended opinion.
"Artificial intelligence will create more problems than it solves for humanity over the next 20 years." Discuss this statement. Consider both supporting and opposing arguments before giving your own conclusion.

Advanced language to aim for: "It is often argued that… Proponents of this view contend… On the other hand… A further consideration is… To conclude, I would say…"

Task 2 — Problem and solution.
Many young people report high levels of stress and anxiety. What do you think are the main causes? What solutions would you propose, and who should be responsible for implementing them?

Structure: Cause 1 + example → Cause 2 → Solution A (who implements?) → Solution B → Personal reflection

Task 3 — Hypothetical discussion.
If you could change one major thing about the education system in your country, what would it be and why? What challenges might arise, and how would you overcome them?

Language focus: Conditionals ("If… were to… it would/could…"), concession ("Although… it is worth noting that…"), hedging ("It might be argued that…")
